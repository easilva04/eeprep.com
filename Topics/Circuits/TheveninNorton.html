Thevenin and Norton Theorems

Overview

Thevenin and Norton theorems are fundamental tools in circuit analysis, enabling the simplification of complex linear circuits. These theorems facilitate the calculation of currents, voltages, and power for varying load conditions by replacing parts of the circuit with their equivalent forms.

Thevenin's Theorem

  • Definition: Any linear two-terminal circuit can be represented by an equivalent voltage source (\(V_{\text{th}}\)) in series with a resistor (\(R_{\text{th}}\)).
  • Steps to Determine the Thevenin Equivalent:
    1. Find \(R_{\text{th}}\): Deactivate all independent sources (replace voltage sources with short circuits and current sources with open circuits) and compute the resistance seen from the terminals.
    2. Find \(V_{\text{th}}\): Measure or calculate the open-circuit voltage across the terminals.

Norton's Theorem

  • Definition: Any linear two-terminal circuit can be represented by an equivalent current source (\(I_{\text{n}}\)) in parallel with a resistor (\(R_{\text{n}}\)).
  • Steps to Determine the Norton Equivalent:
    1. Find \(R_{\text{n}}\): Same as \(R_{\text{th}}\).
    2. Find \(I_{\text{n}}\): Measure or calculate the short-circuit current across the terminals.

Relationship Between Thevenin and Norton Equivalents

Thevenin and Norton equivalents are related through the following equations:

\[ V_{\text{th}} = I_{\text{n}} R_{\text{n}} \]

\[ I_{\text{n}} = \frac{V_{\text{th}}}{R_{\text{th}}} \]

These theorems are essential for simplifying circuit analysis, particularly in power systems, electronic design, and fault analysis. Mastery of Thevenin and Norton transformations enables efficient problem-solving for circuits with varying load conditions.
